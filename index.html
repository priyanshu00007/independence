History of India

since 1947

On 15 August 1947 India became independent from the British Empire following the Independence Movement led by Mahatma Gandhi and his message of nonviolent resistance. The handover of power was overseen by Lord Mountbatten, the last viceroy of India. Jawaharlal Nehru became the first Prime Minister of India and raised the Indian national flag above the Lahori Gate of the Red Fort in Delhi in honour of the celebrations. Independence Day, in India, national holiday celebrated annually on August 15. Independence Day marks the end of British rule in 1947, brought about by the Indian Independence Act of July 18 that year, and the establishment of a free and independent Indian nation. It also marks the anniversary of the partition of the subcontinent into two countries, India and Pakistan, which occurred at midnight on August 14–15, 1947. (In Pakistan, Independence Day is celebrated on August 14.) The East India Company ruled India for 100 years, until it was replaced by direct British rule (often referred to as the British raj) in the wake of the Indian Mutiny in 1857–58. The Indian independence movement began during World War I and was led by Mohandas K. Gandhi, who advocated for a peaceful and nonviolent end to British rule.

Independence Day is marked throughout India with flag-raising ceremonies, drills, and the singing of the Indian national anthem. Additionally, various cultural programs are made available in the state capitals. After the prime minister participates in the flag-raising ceremony at the Red Fort historic monument in Old Delhi, a parade ensues with members of the armed forces and police. The prime minister then delivers a televised address to the country, recounting the major accomplishments of India during the previous year and outlining future challenges and goals. Kite flying has also become an Independence Day tradition, with kites of various sizes, shapes, and colours filling the sky. Also, to commemorate the day, government offices in New Delhi remain lit throughout the holiday, even though they are closed.

Freedom Fighters Who Had fought Against Britishers In Early 1800's

One of the earliest significant instances of organized resistance against British rule in India was the Sepoy Mutiny, also known as the Indian Rebellion of 1857 or the First War of Independence. The rebellion began in Meerut on May 10, 1857, when Indian sepoys (soldiers) of the British East India Company's army revolted against their British officers.

Mangal Pandey (1827–1857) was an Indian sepoy in the British East India Company's army, known for his pivotal role in the lead-up to the Sepoy Mutiny of 1857. His refusal to use the greased cartridges and his attempt to incite rebellion against British officers marked a courageous act of resistance. His execution on April 8, 1857, for his attempted mutiny became a rallying point for discontented Indians, inspiring them to challenge British rule. Mangal Pandey's sacrificial stand and defiance against injustice served as an early symbol of resistance, contributing to the broader movement for India's eventual independence.

Rani Lakshmibai (1828–1858), also known as the Rani of Jhansi, was a prominent leader in the 1857 rebellion against British rule in India. Her fearless participation in battles against British forces, including leading her troops in combat, demonstrated remarkable courage and determination. Her death while fighting on the battlefield elevated her to a symbol of resistance, inspiring future generations in the struggle for India's independence.

Tantia Tope (1814–1859) was a significant figure in the 1857 rebellion against British colonial rule in India. As a key leader, he played a pivotal role in organizing and leading forces in resistance against the British forces. Despite his valiant efforts, he was eventually captured by the British and executed in 1859. Tantia Tope's leadership and commitment made him a symbol of defiance and courage in the struggle for India's independence.

Kunwar Singh (1777–1858) was a notable leader from Bihar who played a vital role in the 1857 revolt against British rule. Leading an armed rebellion, he fought valiantly in multiple battles, embodying the spirit of resistance. Despite sustaining injuries during the conflict, his unwavering determination and leadership remained steadfast. Kunwar Singh's legacy as a courageous leader in the struggle for independence endures, inspiring future generations in the pursuit of India's freedom.

Khudiram Bose (1889–1908) was a precocious revolutionary who displayed remarkable courage at a young age. At 18 years old, he attempted to assassinate a British judge as a protest against colonial oppression. Unfortunately, his mission was unsuccessful, leading to his arrest, trial, and subsequent execution. Khudiram Bose's audacious act highlighted the determination of young Indians in the struggle for independence and left a lasting impact on the nation's fight against British rule.

Madan Lal Dhingra (1883–1909) was a significant figure in the Indian revolutionary movement in London during the early 20th century. In an act of resistance, he assassinated Sir William Curzon Wyllie, a prominent British official, in 1909. This daring action was a statement against British imperialism. Dhingra's capture, trial, and subsequent execution underlined his unwavering commitment to India's struggle for independence and the lengths to which revolutionaries were willing to go to challenge colonial oppression.

Lala Lajpat Rai (1865–1928) was a prominent leader whose substantial contributions spanned the early 1900s. He emerged as a strong voice against British colonialism, advocating for India's independence. In 1928, during protests against the Simon Commission, he confronted brutal police baton charges, sustaining severe injuries that ultimately led to his demise later that year. Lala Lajpat Rai's dedication and sacrifice in the face of oppressive tactics underscored his commitment to India's freedom struggle, making him an enduring symbol of resilience and determination.

Ashfaqulla Khan (1900–1927) and Ram Prasad Bismil (1897–1927) were pivotal figures in the Indian independence movement. As key members of the Hindustan Republican Association, they were instrumental in the Kakori Conspiracy, a daring train robbery aimed at funding revolutionary activities. Their commitment to the cause was unwavering, even in the face of severe consequences. Both Khan and Bismil were executed for their revolutionary efforts, solidifying their status as valiant heroes who sacrificed their lives for India's freedom.

Udham Singh (1899–1940) carried the legacy of resistance from the early 20th century into a powerful act of retribution. His assassination of Michael O'Dwyer, the former Lieutenant Governor of Punjab, in 1940 was a direct response to the Jallianwala Bagh massacre of 1919. Singh's act highlighted the lasting impact of colonial atrocities and his unwavering commitment to justice. His sacrifice and determination serve as a poignant reminder of the unyielding spirit that fueled India's fight for independence.

Birsa Munda, although not directly associated with the 1857 rebellion, emerged as a pivotal tribal leader in the later years of British rule. His leadership was instrumental in advocating for the rights and empowerment of tribal communities. He organized movements against oppressive British policies and exploitative practices, seeking to improve the conditions of tribal people. Birsa Munda's legacy lies in his role as a grassroots leader who championed the rights of marginalized communities, contributing to the broader fabric of resistance against British colonial rule.

Subhas Chandra Bose (1897–1945) was a prominent leader in India's fight for independence. He is best known for his role in leading the Indian National Army (INA), formed with the aim of liberating India from British rule. Bose's charismatic leadership and determination inspired many to join the cause. He sought international support for India's freedom struggle during World War II, seeking alliances with nations like Germany and Japan. His slogan, "Give me blood, and I shall give you freedom," captured the spirit of sacrifice and dedication required for India's independence. Bose's actions and strategies challenged British authority and contributed to a more assertive approach to achieving freedom. Despite his early disappearance in 1945, Bose's legacy as the "Netaji" (Respected Leader) endures, symbolizing the resolute spirit of those who fought for India's liberation.

Bhagat Singh (1907–1931) was a fearless and iconic revolutionary who made a significant impact on India's struggle for independence. As an early advocate of more militant approaches, he sought to awaken the nation's conscience against British oppression. Bhagat Singh's involvement in the Lahore Conspiracy Case, including the bombing of the Central Legislative Assembly in 1929, aimed to protest repressive laws. His acts were characterized by a sense of self-sacrifice and a commitment to a higher cause. Despite his young age, Bhagat Singh's bravery, eloquence, and resolve in courtrooms captured the imagination of the Indian public and energized the fight against colonial rule. His execution on March 23, 1931, at the age of 23, transformed him into a symbol of courage and defiance. Bhagat Singh's legacy continues to inspire generations of Indians to fight for justice, freedom, and equality.

Chandra Shekhar Azad (1906–1931) was a resolute nationalist who played a significant role in India's quest for independence. Known for his fierce determination, Azad actively engaged in revolutionary activities against British colonial rule. He evaded capture by British authorities with his cunning and quick thinking, adopting the name "Azad," which means "free" in Hindi, to symbolize his unwavering commitment to freedom. Azad was a key figure in organizations like the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) and participated in acts of defiance and resistance. Azad's life was tragically cut short in a gunfight with the police in Alfred Park, Allahabad, on February 27, 1931. Rather than surrendering, he chose to end his own life to evade capture. Chandra Shekhar Azad's fierce nationalism, dedication, and ultimate sacrifice make him an enduring symbol of resistance against colonial oppression.

Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel (1875–1950), often referred to as the "Iron Man of India," played a pivotal role in shaping India's post-independence landscape. As an astute statesman and a key leader in the freedom movement, Patel's leadership was instrumental in uniting India. Following independence in 1947, Patel faced the monumental task of integrating over 500 princely states into the newly formed nation. His diplomacy, firmness, and negotiation skills led to the peaceful and voluntary accession of these states to the Indian Union. This immense achievement prevented fragmentation and laid the foundation for a united and diverse India. Patel's dedication to national unity and his pragmatic approach to governance left an indelible mark on the country's early years. His vision and tireless efforts have earned him enduring respect as a founding father of modern India.

Rani Gaidinliu (1915–1993) was a remarkable Naga leader who made significant contributions to both India's struggle for independence and the Naga self-determination movement. Emerging as a spiritual and political figure, she led a movement against British colonial rule, advocating for the rights and autonomy of her people. At a young age, Gaidinliu became a charismatic leader, guiding the Heraka movement—a socio-religious movement centered on Naga cultural revival and resistance against British influences. Her activism caught the attention of the British authorities, leading to her arrest and imprisonment for several years. After India gained independence, Gaidinliu continued her efforts, advocating for the rights and self-determination of the Naga people. She remained a strong advocate for Naga identity, culture, and political representation. Her contributions to Naga society and her dedication to the principles of self-determination and autonomy have made her an enduring symbol of Naga resilience and the broader fight for indigenous rights.

Sarojini Naidu (1879–1949) was a multifaceted leader, renowned as both a poet and an activist, who left an indelible mark on India's struggle for independence. Her eloquence and passion were evident in her poetry, which often captured the spirit of the times. Naidu's involvement in the Indian National Congress and the civil disobedience movement showcased her dedication to the cause of freedom. Naidu's notable achievements extended beyond India's borders. She represented India at international forums and conferences, eloquently presenting the country's aspirations for independence on a global stage. Her efforts in building international awareness and support for India's freedom struggle were invaluable. Known as the "Nightingale of India," Naidu's artistic and political contributions embodied the spirit of resistance and empowerment. Her legacy as a trailblazing woman leader continues to inspire generations of Indians and poets alike.

Dr. B. R. Ambedkar (1891–1956) was a towering figure in India's history, renowned for his multifaceted contributions as a jurist, social reformer, and architect of the Indian Constitution. As a champion of social justice and equality, he dedicated his life to addressing the plight of marginalized and oppressed communities. Ambedkar's role in shaping the Constitution of India was pivotal, ensuring that fundamental rights and protections were enshrined for all citizens. His efforts led to the inclusion of affirmative action measures to uplift historically disadvantaged groups. As a leader, he fought against caste-based discrimination, advocating for the rights of Dalits (formerly known as "untouchables"). His efforts culminated in his embrace of Buddhism, seeking an alternative path for Dalits beyond the confines of caste. Ambedkar's legacy remains influential, symbolizing the struggle for social justice and empowerment. His contributions continue to inspire movements for equality and inclusivity, making him an enduring icon of change and progress.

Movies And Songs Related to Independence

  • Lagaan (2001): Set during the British colonial period, this film portrays a cricket match between Indian villagers and British officers, serving as a metaphor for the struggle against oppression.
  • Rang De Basanti (2006): This film intertwines the stories of contemporary Indian students with the stories of freedom fighters, highlighting their sacrifices and inspiring a call to action.
  • Gandhi (1982): A biographical film depicting the life of Mahatma Gandhi, a central figure in India's independence movement.
  • Shaheed (1965): This film pays tribute to Bhagat Singh, focusing on his revolutionary activities and the sacrifices he made for the country.
  • The Legend of Bhagat Singh (2002): Another film based on the life of Bhagat Singh, shedding light on his contributions to the fight against British colonial rule.
  • Mother India (1957): This classic film portrays the struggles of a rural Indian woman against poverty and adversity, serving as a metaphor for the nation's fight against colonial rule.
  • Krantiveer (1994): A film focusing on corruption and social issues, with themes of patriotism and sacrifice echoing the spirit of independence.
  • Mangal Pandey: The Rising (2005): A historical drama that focuses on the life of Mangal Pandey, a key figure in the events leading up to the Sepoy Mutiny of 1857.
  • Kranti (1981): A multi-starrer film that portrays various aspects of the Indian independence movement, showcasing the struggles and sacrifices of different characters.
  • Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose: The Forgotten Hero (2005): This film portrays the life and struggles of Subhas Chandra Bose, focusing on his efforts to free India from British rule.
  • Sardar (1993): A biographical film on the life of Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, showcasing his efforts in integrating princely states after independence.
  • Gandhi to Hitler (2011): While not focusing solely on Indian independence, this film explores the interactions between Mahatma Gandhi and Adolf Hitler during World War II.
  • Raazi (2018): The film revolves around an Indian spy married to a Pakistani military officer during the 1971 Indo-Pak war, showcasing themes of patriotism and sacrifice.
